Build & Tune Your Kart

Customize every aspect of your kart's performance

Front End Setup

  • Caster: The angle of the kingpin relative to the ground when viewed from the side.
    • Increased Caster: Increases front-end grip and jacking effect throughout the corner (more aggressive turn-in).
    • Decreased Caster: Decreases front-end grip.
  • Camber: The angle of the wheel relative to the vertical axis when viewed from the front.
    • Negative Camber: Top of tire leans in; can improve turn-in but may reduce mid-corner grip.
    • Positive Camber: Top of tire leans out; often used on right front to maximize contact patch under cornering load.
  • Toe: The angle of the front wheels relative to each other (parallel, pointing in, or pointing out).
    • Toe Out: Wheels point slightly outward; increases responsiveness and turn-in (most common).
    • Toe In: Wheels point slightly inward; rarely used.
  • Ackermann Steering Geometry: The relationship between the steering angles of the inside and outside front wheels.
    • Increased Ackermann: Inside wheel turns more than outside; generally for tighter, slower corners.
    • Decreased Ackermann: Inside and outside wheels turn similarly; generally for faster, sweeping corners.
  • Front Ride Height: Adjusted by moving spacers on the spindles.
    • Raising Front Ride Height (Lowering Spindles): Increases front-end grip (more weight transfer).
    • Lowering Front Ride Height (Raising Spindles): Decreases front-end grip.
  • Front Track Width: Adjusted by moving spacers on the spindles.
    • Wider Front Track: Increases front-end bite/grip and responsiveness (good for a "tight" kart).
    • Narrower Front Track: Decreases front-end bite (good for a "loose" kart).

Rear End Setup

  • Rear Track Width: Adjusted by moving rear hubs or spacers on the axle.
    • Narrower Rear Track: Increases weight transfer and lift of inside-rear wheel (more "jacks" the kart, increasing rotation/bite).
    • Wider Rear Track: Decreases rear grip and rotation (good for a kart that is too "loose" or "free").
  • Rear Axle Height: Adjusted by moving bearing hangers.
    • Raising Rear Ride Height: Increases weight transfer and rear grip/rotation (more jacking).
    • Lowering Rear Ride Height: Decreases weight transfer and rear grip.
  • Rear Axle Hardness: Axles come in different materials/thicknesses (soft, medium, hard).
    • Harder Axle: Reduces flex, providing more stability and freeing up the kart.
    • Softer Axle: Increases flex, increasing rear grip and rotation.
  • Torsion Bars/Stiffeners (Rear): Inserting or removing bars.
    • Installing/Stiffening: Increases chassis rigidity (useful on high-grip tracks but can cause understeer if too stiff).
    • Removing/Loosening: Increases chassis flex, increasing grip on low-grip or bumpy surfaces.

Weight & Balance

  • Nose Weight Percentage: The percentage of total weight on the front axle.
    • Increase Nose Weight: Increases front-end grip/bite.
    • Decrease Nose Weight: Decreases front-end grip/bite.
  • Left Side Weight Percentage: The percentage of total weight on the left side.
    • Increase Left Side Weight: Shifts weight to the left (crucial for turning left on an oval). Increases left-side load and overall side bite.
  • Cross Weight Percentage (Bite): The diagonal weight distribution (Right-Front + Left-Rear vs. total weight).
    • Increase Cross Weight: Generally increases lift on inside-rear tire and makes kart tighter (less prone to sliding).
    • Decrease Cross Weight: Makes the kart looser (more prone to sliding) and easier to steer.
  • Driver/Seat Position: Adjusting seat forward/backward and vertical height.
    • Moving Seat Forward: Increases nose weight percentage, adding front grip.
    • Moving Seat Backward: Decreases nose weight percentage, adding rear grip/traction.
    • Raising Seat (Higher CG): Increases weight transfer, which can add grip in low-grip conditions.
    • Lowering Seat (Lower CG): Reduces weight transfer, making the kart more stable.

Chassis Stiffness

  • Front Torsion Bars/Stiffeners: Inserting or removing the bar.
    • Installing/Stiffening Bars: Increases chassis rigidity (useful on high-grip tracks but can cause understeer if too stiff).
    • Removing/Loosening Bars: Increases chassis flex, increasing grip on low-grip or bumpy surfaces.

Tire Adjustments

  • Tire Pressure (PSI): Varying pressure in all four tires to tune for track grip and heat.
    • Left Side vs. Right Side: Right-side tires often run higher pressure than left side to help the kart turn and manage heat.
    • Front vs. Rear: Adjustments fine-tune the balance of front-end vs. rear-end grip.
  • Tire Stagger: The difference in circumference (or diameter) between tires on the same axle.
    • Rear Stagger (Right Rear > Left Rear): Increase makes the kart turn left more aggressively. Decrease makes the kart track straighter (good for fast, sweeping tracks).
    • Front Stagger (Right Front > Left Front): Increase helps the kart turn into the corner.
  • Tire Compound Selection: Choosing the correct tire compound (soft, medium, hard) for track conditions and temperature.
    • Soft Compound: More grip in cold/low-traction conditions.
    • Hard Compound: Better for hot/high-traction conditions.
  • Tire Prep/Conditioning: Applying chemical treatments to soften rubber, increase grip, or pull moisture from track surface.
    • Tire Grooming/Siping: Cutting grooves or sipes into tire surface to create biting edges for increased forward and side grip.

Drivetrain & Engine

  • Gearing (Sprocket/Clutch Changes): Changing the ratio between the engine clutch and the rear axle sprocket.
    • Smaller Axle Sprocket (Taller Gear): Higher top speed, slower acceleration (good for a long straight).
    • Larger Axle Sprocket (Shorter Gear): Better acceleration off the corner, lower top speed (good for tight tracks).
  • Clutch Engagement RPM: Adjusting the clutch springs or weights to change the engine RPM at which the clutch engages.
    • Higher Engagement RPM: Delivers a harder "hit" of power, often beneficial.
    • Lower Engagement RPM: Smoother power delivery.
  • Clutch Material/Type: Drum or disc clutches require different tuning for heat and track condition.
    • Drum Clutch: Simpler, good for beginners.
    • Disc Clutch: More tunable, better for competitive racing.
  • Front Sprocket/Clutch Bell: Teeth adjustment for drivetrain ratio.
  • Carburetor Jetting: High-speed and low-speed jet tuning.
  • Fuel/Oil Mixture: Proper ratios for your engine.
  • Ignition Timing: Advance or retard timing, spark plug heat range.
  • Exhaust System: Pipe length and volume for performance.

Braking System

  • Brake Pad Compound: Different compounds for various conditions.
  • Brake Disc Type/Material: Vented/solid, steel/cast iron.
  • Brake Bias: Front/rear split for balanced stopping.
  • Master Cylinder/Caliper: Piston size adjustments.
  • Pedal Ratio/Feel: Adjusting pedal position and response.
